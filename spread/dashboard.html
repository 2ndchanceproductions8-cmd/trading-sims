How the model reads a spread

Each team's scoring rate and rate-allowed come from ESPN's standings, then get regressed toward the league average (a hot week isn't a rate). Offense meets defense: exp_home = home_off × away_def / lg_avg, same for the away side; the difference plus a per-league home-field edge is the expected margin. A normal curve of per-league width sigma around that margin gives P(cover) against the posted number, compared to the vig-free price. Whole spreads can push.
CLV is the real scoreboard. Bankroll over a few dozen bets is mostly noise. Whether this bot got a better number than the market closed at — the “beat the close” column — is what separates an edge from a lucky month. The honest prior is that it comes back negative: the market is very hard to beat, and this bot is built to find that out cheaply.
How the close is captured: ESPN deletes an event's odds block the moment the game goes final, so a closing line cannot be recovered at settlement — the first three bets settled with no CLV at all for exactly that reason. A separate snapshot run now records each pending bet's line in the hours before tip and keeps the latest one, and every row says how long before tip it was taken. Anything marked snapshot T−20m is close to a real close; T−400m is a morning line wearing the label, and is why the lag is printed instead of hidden. Line source: none — all 2 settled bets pre-date the snapshot run, so they have no closing line to be measured against and are excluded from the CLV cards above rather than counted as a miss.
